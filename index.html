📊 The Mathematical Modelling Cycle

We need maths, not guesses. But before we calculate, we need to set up the problem. That is what the modelling cycle is for.

Modelling Focus — Phone Plans: Class Worked Example

Follow along as your teacher works through this. You will use the same four steps for your own scenario in the Practice tab.

Step 1 — Formulate

What information do we need? Monthly cost of each plan at a given number of texts.
Assumption we're making: The person only uses texts (no data, no extra calls).
What we're trying to find: For what number of texts per month is each plan cheaper?

Plan A cost formula: If texts ≤ 100 → $35. If texts > 100 → $35 + (texts − 100) × $0.15
Plan B cost formula: Always $45 (flat rate, no matter how many texts)
Step 2 — Solve: Three Usage Cases
Texts / month Plan A cost Plan B cost Cheaper option
60 texts ? $45 ?
100 texts ? $45 ?
200 texts ? $45 ?
60 texts: Plan A = $35 (under 100 free texts, no extra charge).
100 texts: Plan A = $35 (exactly 100 — no extra charge yet).
200 texts: Plan A = $35 + (200 − 100) × $0.15 = $35 + $15 = $50.
Step 3 — Interpret: What Does This Mean?

Plan A is cheaper when you send 100 or fewer texts. Plan B is cheaper when you send more than 100 texts. So the better plan depends entirely on how many texts you send each month.

📐 The Crossover Point

The crossover point is where both plans cost the same. We need to find where Plan A cost = $45. If texts > 100, Plan A = $35 + (texts − 100) × $0.15. Setting this equal to $45 and solving gives the crossover point.

$35 + (t − 100) × 0.15 = $45
(t − 100) × 0.15 = $10
t − 100 = 10 ÷ 0.15 ≈ 66.7
t ≈ 166.7 → so at 167 texts/month, both plans cost the same (≈$45.05).
Under 167 texts → Plan A wins. Over 167 texts → Plan B wins.
Step 4 — Communicate: The Recommendation + Generalising Rule
Recommendation: "Choose Plan A if you send fewer than 167 texts per month. Choose Plan B if you send more than 167 texts per month. If you are unsure how many texts you send, Plan B gives you peace of mind at a fixed price."

Assumption stated: "We assumed the person uses texts only, not data. If they also use data, this comparison would need extra calculations."
📐 The Generalising Rule

Pattern: As the number of texts increases beyond the free allowance, Plan A becomes more expensive at a rate of $0.15 per text. Plan B stays flat at $45. The crossover happens when the extra charges on Plan A equal the $10 price difference between the plans.

Rule in words: "Plan A is better when texts ≤ 167. Plan B is better when texts > 167."
Rule mathematically: Crossover = 100 + (45 − 35) ÷ 0.15 ≈ 167 texts.

📌 Before moving to PracticeAsk: "What would change this answer?" (e.g. different flat rate, different per-text charge, a different free allowance.) A good model identifies what the answer depends on. Then send students to the Practice tab to try this with a new scenario.

Practice — Choose Your Scenario

Choose one of the three scenarios below. Work through the four steps: Formulate → Solve → Interpret → Communicate. After the core comparison, complete the generalising prompt — everyone does this part.

Scenario A
⚡ Two Electricity Providers
The Nguyen family wants to choose the best electricity provider. Help them decide.
Provider A — PowerDirect
28c per kilowatt-hour (kWh)
No daily supply charge
No exit fees
Provider B — EnergyPlus
22c per kWh
$1.20 per day supply charge
No exit fees
📋 Step 1 — Formulate first

Before calculating: write down what information you need, what you are assuming, and what you are trying to find. A 30-day month is a reasonable assumption to start with.

Step 2 — Solve: Calculate the monthly cost at each usage level
Monthly usage (kWh) Provider A cost ($) Provider B cost ($) Cheaper option
200 kWh
400 kWh
600 kWh
Provider B has two separate charges you need to add together for each month. Think about what changes as you use more electricity — and what stays the same regardless of usage. Write a formula for Provider B's monthly cost before you substitute any numbers.
Provider A: usage × $0.28 only.
Provider B: (usage × $0.22) + ($1.20 × 30 days) = (usage × $0.22) + $36

200 kWh: A = $56 | B = $44 + $36 = $80 → A cheaper
400 kWh: A = $112 | B = $88 + $36 = $124 → A cheaper
600 kWh: A = $168 | B = $132 + $36 = $168 → tie — this is the crossover!
Step 3 — Interpret + Step 4 — Communicate

Once you have your table filled in: write two sentences. (1) Which provider is better and under what conditions? (2) What assumption did you make that could change your recommendation?

Scenario B
🛒 Supermarket Loyalty Deals
The Park family shops for groceries weekly. Which supermarket loyalty deal saves them more money?
SuperMart — FreshRewards
Earn 1 point per $1 spent
500 points = $5 voucher
No joining fee
Points expire after 12 months
ValueGrocer — SaveMore
4% cashback on all purchases
Minimum $200/month to qualify
Cashback paid quarterly
No joining fee
📋 Step 1 — Formulate first

Before calculating: think about how the FreshRewards points convert to dollars. How many dollars of shopping does it take to earn a $5 voucher? Express both deals as a percentage saving — this makes them directly comparable.

Step 2 — Solve: Annual saving at each spend level
Monthly spend ($) FreshRewards annual saving SaveMore annual saving Better deal
$100/month
$250/month
$400/month
The two deals use different systems — one uses points, the other uses a percentage. Before you can compare them, it helps to convert the points deal into an equivalent percentage. Think: earning 500 points requires spending $500, which gives back $5. What percentage of $500 is $5? Once you have both as percentages, you can compare them directly.
FreshRewards: 500 points = $500 spend = $5 back = 1% saving.
SaveMore: 4% cashback (but only if spending ≥ $200/month).

$100/month (annual = $1,200): FreshRewards = $12 | SaveMore = $0 (doesn't qualify) → FreshRewards wins
$250/month (annual = $3,000): FreshRewards = $30 | SaveMore = $120 → SaveMore wins
$400/month (annual = $4,800): FreshRewards = $48 | SaveMore = $192 → SaveMore wins
Scenario C
🎒 Rent vs Buy — Sports Equipment
The school runs a kayaking program. Should they rent kayaks each term or buy them outright?
Option A — Rent
$35 per kayak per day
No storage or maintenance costs
Available whenever needed
No upfront cost
Option B — Buy
$480 per kayak (upfront)
$15/year maintenance per kayak
Storage space needed
Lasts approximately 8 years
📋 Step 1 — Formulate first

This comparison spans multiple years. Decide: will you compare cost per year, or total cost over the 8-year lifespan? State your assumption clearly — it will affect your answer. The school uses 4 kayaks.

Step 2 — Solve: Compare costs (for 4 kayaks, per year)
Days used per year Rent total cost (4 kayaks) Buy total cost (4 kayaks, year 1) Better option
5 days/year
20 days/year
40 days/year
The "buy" option has a large upfront cost that is paid only once, then smaller ongoing costs each year. Think about how you would spread the purchase price across the years it will be used — this is sometimes called an annual cost. What annual figure do you get when you divide the purchase price by the lifespan, then add the yearly maintenance?
Rent cost (4 kayaks, n days): 4 × $35 × n = $140n
Buy cost (4 kayaks, annualised over 8 years): (4 × $480)/8 + (4 × $15) = $240 + $60 = $300/year

5 days: Rent = $700 | Buy = $300 → Buy cheaper
20 days: Rent = $2,800 | Buy = $300 → Buy much cheaper
40 days: Rent = $5,600 | Buy = $300 → Buy far cheaper

Note: if the school only had 1–2 days of use per year, rent becomes competitive: crossover ≈ 300 ÷ 140 ≈ 2.1 days/year.

E1 — Find the Exact Break-Even Point

For your scenario, find the exact value of the variable (texts, kWh, days, etc.) where both options cost exactly the same. Show your algebraic or systematic working — don't just guess.

Write the cost formula for each option using a variable (like n for the quantity). Set them equal to each other. What value of n makes them the same? Think about what operation isolates n — you're essentially solving for the point where the two expressions are equal.
E2 — Design Your Own

Create a new financial comparison problem of your own — it must involve at least one fixed cost and one variable cost. Write the two options, solve it for three usage levels, find the break-even point, and write the generalising rule. Be ready to explain your problem to the class in 2 minutes.

★1 — Three-Variable Phone Plan

Plan A now offers a bonus: 1GB of free data for every month you send 100 texts or fewer. Data elsewhere costs $12/GB. How does this change your generalising rule from the warm-up?

Investigate: at what number of texts does the combined value of Plan A (cheap plan + free data bonus) equal Plan B? Write your new generalising rule.

The data bonus only applies in months when you send 100 texts or fewer. Think of Plan A's effective cost in those months as the plan cost minus the value of the free data. How does subtracting $12 from Plan A's monthly cost change the crossover calculation?
★2 — Write a Recommendation

Write a financial recommendation to a fictional family — the Nguyens — who are trying to decide between two electricity providers. Your recommendation must include: (a) the generalising rule with the crossover point, (b) at least two assumptions you made, (c) a statement about how the recommendation changes if one assumption changes, and (d) a conclusion that connects to the idea of making wise rather than just cheap choices.

This last point connects to our unit's Catholic Perspectives theme — stewardship isn't just about minimising cost, it's about making responsible, informed decisions.
